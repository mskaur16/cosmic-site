What is Astronomy?

Astronomy is one of the oldest sciences, yet it is also one of the most rapidly advancing.
Ancient civilizations tracked planetary motions for calendars and navigation.
However, the modern era began when telescopic observations revealed that Earth is not the center of the cosmos.

The paradigm shift initiated by heliocentric theory eventually led to a deeper realization: our Sun is one star among billions in the Milky Way, and our galaxy is one among billions in the observable universe.
The 20th century introduced an even more revolutionary idea — the universe itself is expanding.

What is Hubble’s Law and the Expanding Universe?

In 1929, Edwin Hubble published results showing that distant galaxies exhibit redshift — a shift in spectral lines toward longer wavelengths.
This redshift increases proportionally with distance.

This relationship, now called Hubble’s Law, is expressed as:

v=H0​d

  • v is the recession velocity of a galaxy
  • d is its distance
  • H0is the Hubble constant.

Redshift indicates that space itself is expanding.
Galaxies are not moving through space away from us; rather, space between galaxies is stretching.
This discovery implied that the universe had a beginning in time — leading to the development of the Big Bang model.

Modern measurements from missions such as the Planck satellite have refined the value of the Hubble constant and constrained cosmological parameters with high precision.

What is The Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation?

If the universe began in a hot, dense state, residual radiation from that era should still exist.
In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson detected uniform microwave radiation coming from all directions in space.

This radiation, known as the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), represents light released approximately 380,000 years after the Big Bang, when electrons and protons combined to form neutral atoms and the universe became transparent.

Tiny temperature fluctuations in the CMB correspond to density variations that later formed galaxies and large-scale structures.
Observations from missions such as WMAP and Planck mapped these fluctuations with extraordinary accuracy.

The CMB is one of the strongest pieces of evidence supporting the Big Bang model.

What are Dark Matter and Galactic Rotation?

Observations of galactic rotation curves revealed a major discrepancy.
Stars in galaxies orbit at speeds inconsistent with the visible mass present.
According to Newtonian gravity, outer stars should orbit more slowly than inner ones.
Instead, their speeds remain roughly constant.

This anomaly suggests the presence of unseen mass — dark matter.

Dark matter does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, but its gravitational effects are measurable.
Gravitational lensing and galaxy cluster dynamics further confirm its existence.
Current estimates suggest that dark matter constitutes approximately 27% of the total energy density of the universe.

What is the Dark Energy and Accelerated Expansion?

In 1998, two independent teams studying Type Ia supernovae discovered that cosmic expansion is accelerating.
This unexpected result implied the presence of a repulsive component of energy — now called dark energy.

Dark energy appears to make up roughly 68% of the universe’s total energy density.
Its nature remains unknown, though it is often modeled as a cosmological constant in Einstein’s field equations.

The discovery fundamentally altered cosmological models and raised profound questions about the ultimate fate of the universe.

What are some challenges in Astronomy?

Despite remarkable progress, several major questions remain:

  • What is the true nature of dark matter?
  • What is dark energy?
  • Why is there tension between different measurements of the Hubble constant?
  • What occurred during cosmic inflation?

Future observatories such as the James Webb Space Telescope are providing deeper observations of early galaxies, potentially refining models of cosmic evolution.

What can we conclude from this?

Modern astronomy has transitioned from descriptive sky mapping to quantitative cosmology.
Observations of redshift, the Cosmic Microwave Background, galactic rotation curves, and supernovae collectively support a universe that began in a hot, dense state and continues to expand at an accelerating rate.

The combination of theory and observation has transformed astronomy into a precision science.
Yet the dominance of dark matter and dark energy reminds us that most of the universe remains fundamentally mysterious.
Continued advancements in observational technology and theoretical modeling are essential for addressing these unresolved questions.

The universe is not static, not simple, and not fully understood — and that is precisely what makes astronomy one of the most dynamic and profound scientific disciplines.
